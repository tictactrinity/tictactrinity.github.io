\## How Theology in Practice Reveals the Difference Between Waiting and Forcing the End



\*\*By Fasih Rana, March 12, 2026\*\*



In the opening months of 2026, the Middle East erupted into a conflict that has drawn in the United States, Israel, Iran, and the Gulf Arab states. But beneath the surface of missile strikes and diplomatic manoeuvring lies a deeper question that has shaped this war from the beginning:



\*\*When leaders and commanders invoke prophecy to justify action, are they witnessing the unfolding of divine will—or are they simply executing a political plan and calling it scripture?\*\*



The distinction matters more than ever. Across the three Abrahamic faiths, the gap between what sacred texts teach and how adherents act in this conflict reveals a fundamental divide: some wait for prophecy; others try to force it.



\---



\## The Theological Stop Sign



In the Quran, there is a verse that stands as a direct command against the very kind of "apocalypse accelerationism" now on display in the Middle East.



> \*\*"Man was created of haste. I will show you My signs, so do not impatiently urge Me."\*\* — Quran 21:37 \[1]



The classical commentators understood this as a definitive prohibition. Shaykh Abdul-Rahim Reasat explains that the verse describes humanity as "hasty and impatient—as though he has been made from hastiness and that it is an essential part of him." The context is crucial: "There have been people who have demanded miracles and signs from the Prophets instantly. Some demanded that the punishments they were threatened with be manifested instantly. This verse shows that Allah does things according to His own time frame, and their demands will not bring the results they insist on seeing instantly" \[1].



This is not a minor theological footnote. It is a structural restraint built into Islamic scripture. The believer is commanded to wait, to trust divine timing, and—critically—\*\*not to demand that God hasten His signs\*\*.



No equivalent verse exists in Jewish or Christian scripture. While Jewish tradition contains the "Three Oaths" in the Talmud—interpreted by some as a prohibition against ascending to the Land of Israel by force—these are debated, contested, and read differently by Zionist and anti-Zionist Jews. Christianity's New Testament instructs believers to "watch" and "endure" (Matthew 24), but offers no explicit command against attempting to hasten the end.



The absence of such a textual stop has consequences.

\## "Anointed by Jesus to Light the Signal Fire"



In late February 2026, the Military Religious Freedom Foundation (MRFF)—a watchdog group that tracks religious coercion in the US military—began receiving complaints. By March 2, they had documented more than 110 reports from service members across 50 military installations \[2].



The content of those complaints is startling.



According to an anonymous non-commissioned officer, a commander told troops that the war with Iran was "all part of God's divine plan." The commander reportedly cited the Book of Revelation and claimed the conflict would lead to Armageddon and the "imminent return of Jesus Christ." Most strikingly, the commander allegedly stated: \*\*"President Trump has been anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon and mark his return to Earth"\*\* \[2].



The Baptist Standard, a Christian publication, reacted with alarm. "It never occurred to me when I was being taught dispensationalism as a child and teenager that that interpretation of Revelation would ever be official U.S. military doctrine," the editorial board wrote. "We ought to be concerned by such a hubristic assertion. And if a U.S. military commander really did instruct other officers to disseminate that claim, we ought to be concerned about how our military is being motivated to go into battle" \[3].



Russell Moore, writing for Christianity Today, observed that prophecy charts have become remarkably malleable. "When these things turn out not to be so clear after all, none of the prophecy teachers ever says, 'Well, I was wrong. Let me go back to the Scriptures and see where I failed.' They usually just move to the next confident set of assertions." More troubling, Moore notes, that "the answer to 'What will lead to the second coming of Christ?' always lines up with whatever their political tribe supports and can change as fast as that changes" \[4].



The Pentagon has not confirmed or denied the MRFF reports. But the very fact that such allegations circulate—that US service members believe their commanders are framing this war as a necessary step toward Armageddon—reveals how Christian apocalyptic theology has, in practice, become a motivation for aggressive action.

\## The Red Heifer and the Third Temple



Three thousand miles east, another form of apocalyptic preparation is underway.



On the crest of a hill in the occupied West Bank, five Red Angus cows graze quietly. Around them, a group of Israeli settlers watch with anticipation. These are no ordinary cattle. They are perfect red heifers, imported from Texas and reared in special conditions to maintain their purity—without a single blemish, not a stray white or black hair, never placed under a yoke \[5].



According to Jewish tradition, the ashes of a perfectly red heifer are required for the ritual purification necessary to build the Third Temple in Jerusalem. That temple, according to Jewish groups, must be constructed on the raised plateau known to Jews as the Temple Mount and to Muslims as Haram al-Sharif—where the Al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock stand today. Some believe this will herald the arrival of the Messiah \[5].



In February 2026, a few dozen Israelis gathered at a conference in the Shilo settlement to discuss the religious importance of the cows. The first two speakers stood at the lectern with assault rifles slung around their shoulders. "Hezbollah found out about this event and have been talking about it on Telegram," Kobi Mamo, head of the ancient Shiloh archaeological site, said in his opening remarks \[5].



The Temple Institute, which organized the conference, has been searching for a red heifer that fits the Torah's description for years. The perfect cow hasn't been seen for 2,000 years—not since the Romans destroyed the Second Temple in 70 CE. Now, for the first time in two millennia, the possibility exists \[5].



The implications are explosive. Building the Third Temple would require demolishing or relocating the Al-Aqsa Mosque, the third holiest site in Islam. As one analyst warned, "If this were to happen, then the one billion Muslims around the world would be obliged to seek vengeance; it would be a cataclysm that is unimaginable" \[5].



Yet the activists continue their preparations. Billboards in Jerusalem have reportedly declared "Cyrus the Great Is Alive" in reference to Donald Trump—comparing the American president to the Persian king who allowed the Jewish people to return from exile and rebuild the Second Temple \[6]. The message is unmistakable: Trump is seen by some as a divinely ordained instrument for Jewish redemption and temple restoration.

\## The Defensive: Iran's Restraint Under Attack



On February 26, 2026—just two days before the US-Israeli strike that killed Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei—Iranian officials were in Geneva for indirect nuclear negotiations with the United States.



Ali Shamkhani, a senior adviser to Khamenei, posted on X that the "non-development of nuclear weapons is fully consistent with Khamenei's fatwa (religious edict) prohibiting the development of weapons of mass destruction" \[7].



President Masoud Pezeshkian was even more explicit. He described claims that Tehran was seeking nuclear weapons as "lies," insisting that the country would not develop such arms. He cited the same religious edict by Khamenei banning weapons of mass destruction \[7].



This was not a new position. The 2015 fatwa had been reiterated for years. But its timing—days before the attack that would kill the authority who issued it—is crucial. Iran was publicly, repeatedly stating that its nuclear program was under a religious prohibition. And when the strike came, killing Khamenei, Iran's response was defensive. It retaliated against US and Israeli assets, and in doing so struck Gulf states that host US military infrastructure \[8].



But here is what Iran did not do: It did not launch a first strike. It did not act on its Mahdist doctrine to initiate a world-ending conflagration. Despite having an institutionalized apocalyptic theology—the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) is constitutionally tasked with "paving the way for the Mahdi"—Iran's actions were retaliatory, not pre-emptive \[8].



The distinction is everything. Iran holds apocalyptic beliefs. It did not act on them aggressively.

\## The Gulf States: Restraint Under Fire



If Iran's behaviour demonstrates that apocalyptic doctrine does not automatically produce aggression, the Gulf Arab states provide even stronger evidence.



Since the US-Israeli strikes began, hundreds of Iranian drones and missiles have been intercepted across Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries, including Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait, and Oman \[9]. Civilian infrastructure has been targeted—airports, oil facilities, desalination plants. Three civilians were killed and 78 injured in the UAE \[9].



Yet none of these Gulf states has retaliated against Iran.



Anna Jacobs, a non-resident fellow at the Arab Gulf States Institute, observes that the countries of the region "are confronting a shared national security threat" and are "all signalling that they are working to find off-ramps and to de-escalate as much as they can" \[9].



Ali Bakir, an assistant professor at Qatar University, notes that Gulf governments "largely rely on their individual defense capabilities while expressing strong support for one another." Their calculations, he says, "are based on the understanding that they are equipped to defend themselves and can absorb the negative financial and economic implications of the conflict" \[9].



Most importantly, Bakir adds: "The GCC states will continue to urge all involved parties to halt hostilities and return to the negotiation table" \[9].



These are not the actions of nations eager to fulfil apocalyptic prophecy. They are the actions of states protecting their populations, defending their infrastructure, and seeking diplomatic off-ramps—despite being directly attacked.

\## Pakistan: Nuclear Capability, Diplomatic Restraint



Then there is Pakistan.



Possessing the world's fifth-largest nuclear arsenal and bound by a Strategic Mutual Defence Agreement with Saudi Arabia, Pakistan had every reason to respond when Gulf states were hit. It did not.



Instead, Pakistan chose mediation.



Deputy Prime Minister Ishaq Dar told the Senate on March 3 that Islamabad had been in close contact with multiple countries to encourage a return to dialogue. "Through dialogue, a path of understanding can emerge," he said \[10].



Dar also revealed crucial backchannel diplomacy. He said Tehran had sought assurances that Saudi territory would not be used against Iran. "They asked for some assurances… that Saudi soil should not be used. I got them the assurances," Dar told senators, adding that Iran's restrained posture toward Saudi Arabia and Oman reflected those understandings \[10].



Pakistan's Chief of Army Staff, Field Marshal Asim Munir, halted his return journey from the United States to hold consultations in Türkiye. The Pakistani delegation urged Iran to resolve matters through diplomacy \[10].



All of this occurred while Pakistan's Defence Minister, Khawaja Asif, publicly alleged that the war was "orchestrated by the Zionists" \[11]. The rhetoric was sharp—but the response was measured. Pakistan used its nuclear capability to enable restraint, not aggression.

\## The Pattern: Who Acts, Who Waits?



When the actions of all parties are laid side by side, a clear pattern emerges.



| Party | Apocalyptic Belief | Aggressive Action |

| :--- | :--- | :--- |

| US (Christian commanders) | Yes (explicit Armageddon framing) \[2] | \*\*Yes (first strike)\*\* \[2] |

| Israel (Jewish activists/government) | Yes (messianic, Temple preparations) \[5]\[6] | \*\*Yes (co-belligerent)\*\* \[8] |

| Iran (Shia Islam) | Yes (Mahdist doctrine) \[8] | \*\*No (defensive only)\*\* \[8] |

| Gulf States (Sunni Islam) | No evidence \[9] | \*\*No (restraint under attack)\*\* \[9] |

| Pakistan (Sunni Islam) | No evidence \[10] | \*\*No (mediation)\*\* \[10] |



The pattern is not accidental. It reflects the presence or absence of a theological stop sign.



Islamic scripture contains in Quran 21:37 an explicit command to wait, not to demand haste \[1]. The verse does not merely suggest patience; it labels haste as a human flaw and commands believers not to urge God. This structural restraint shaped the behaviour of Iran (defensive retaliation), the Gulf states (restraint under attack), and Pakistan (mediation despite nuclear capability).



Jewish and Christian scripture contain no such command. And in this conflict, adherents of those faiths—US commanders telling troops they are fighting for Armageddon \[2], Israeli activists preparing the red heifer and building toward a Third Temple \[5]—have translated belief into aggressive action.

\## Prophecy or Plan?



The question at the heart of this investigation is simple: When someone claims to be fulfilling prophecy, are they witnessing divine will—or executing a human plan?



The evidence points to a clear answer.



\*\*A plan is when you act to make the prophecy come true.\*\* You launch the war. You build the temple. You prepare the red heifer. You tell troops they are "anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire." You are not waiting for God's timing; you are forcing your own.



\*\*Prophecy, by contrast, is recognized only by those who wait.\*\* It comes upon them suddenly, as the Quran says—not because they orchestrated it, but because they were present when it arrived. The one who waits does not demand signs; the one who waits does not force God's hand; the one who waits can truly say, when events unfold, that they witnessed something beyond human control.



Russell Moore captured this distinction perfectly: "The kingdom of God—present already but not yet fulfilled—tells us what to care about (justice, peace, the poor, the vulnerable) while also shielding us from the disillusionment or bloodthirstiness that can come with expecting to have to bring the fullness of that kingdom on our own" \[4].



The one who acts to force the end is not a prophet. They are a project manager.



And in 2026, the project managers are on one side—while those who wait, defend, and restrain are on the other.
