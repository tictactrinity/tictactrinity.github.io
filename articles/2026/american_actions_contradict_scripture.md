# [Articles](/articles/) > [2026](/articles/2026/) > Command to Wait, Trigger-Happy Plan of Action: How American Ambitions Go Against Prophecy

**By Fasih Rana, March 12, 2026**

In the opening months of 2026, the United States launched a war against Iran that has drawn in Israel and the Gulf Arab states, killing Iran's Supreme Leader and plunging the region into chaos. But as American commanders tell troops they are fighting for Armageddon and officials frame the conflict in biblical terms, a fundamental question arises: **Does any of this align with what Christian scripture actually teaches?**

The answer, based on the words of Jesus himself, is a resounding no. What we are witnessing is not prophecy fulfilled, but a human plan disguised as divine will—a plan that contradicts the very text its proponents claim to follow.

---

## 1. Where Christian Scripture Commands Waiting and Watching

The New Testament is remarkably clear on how believers should approach the end times. The message is consistent: **watch, wait, and tend to your duties—but do not attempt to force God's hand.**

### The Core Teaching: Mark 13

In Mark chapter 13, Jesus' disciples ask for a sign of the end times. His response is instructive:

> **"No one knows about that day or hour, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father. Be on guard! Be alert! You do not know when that time will come. It's like a man going away: He leaves his house in charge of his servants, each with his assigned task, and tells the one at the door to keep watch. Therefore keep watch because you do not know when the owner of the house will come back—whether in the evening, or at midnight, or when the rooster crows, or at dawn. If he comes suddenly, do not let him find you sleeping. What I say to you, I say to everyone: 'Watch!'"** [1]

The passage establishes several key principles:

| Principle | Scriptural Basis |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Knowledge is withheld** | "No one knows... not even the angels... nor the Son, but only the Father" |
| **Watchfulness, not action** | "Be on guard! Be alert!" — the command is to observe, not to act |
| **Attend to assigned tasks** | Each servant has work to do while waiting |
| **Do not sleep** | Remain awake and aware, but do not force the master's return |

### The Warning Against Speculation

Theological commentators have long understood that the withholding of knowledge about the end times serves a purpose. As one analysis notes, "The times and seasons God has put in His own power. And why has not God given us this knowledge? Because we would not make a right use of it if He did. A condition of things would result from this knowledge among our people that would greatly retard the work of God" [2].

Jesus explicitly warned against the very mindset now on display: "Take heed to yourselves, lest at any time your hearts be overcharged with surfeiting, and drunkenness, and cares of this life, and so that day come upon you unawares" (Luke 21:34) [3].

### The Parable of the Faithful Servant

Perhaps most damning for those who would use the end times to justify aggression is Jesus' warning about what happens when servants decide the master is delayed:

> **"But and if that evil servant shall say in his heart, My lord delayeth his coming; And shall begin to smite his fellowservants, and to eat and drink with the drunken; The lord of that servant shall come in a day when he looketh not for him, and in an hour that he is not aware of, And shall cut him asunder, and appoint him his portion with the hypocrites: there shall be weeping and gnashing of teeth."** (Matthew 24:48-51) [4]

The application is unmistakable: those who use the "delay" of the end to justify striking others face divine judgment. Yet this is precisely what is occurring today—with human leaders striking nations and calling it prophecy.

## 2. How American Actions Violate Christian Scripture

The contrast between what scripture commands and what American commanders are doing could not be starker.

### The Reality on the Ground

In late February 2026, the Military Religious Freedom Foundation (MRFF)—a watchdog group tracking religious coercion in the US military—began receiving complaints. By March 2, they had documented **more than 200 complaints from service members across 50 military installations** [5].

The content of those complaints is staggering:

> An anonymous non-commissioned officer reported a commander telling troops that the war with Iran was "all part of God's divine plan." The commander reportedly cited the **Book of Revelation** and claimed the conflict would lead to Armageddon and the "imminent return of Jesus Christ." Most strikingly, the commander allegedly stated: **"President Trump has been anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon and mark his return to Earth"** [5].

Another complainant wrote that their commander "urged us to tell our troops that this was 'all part of God's divine plan' and he specifically referenced numerous citations out of the [Biblical] Book of Revelation referring to Armageddon and the imminent return of Jesus Christ" [5].

Mikey Weinstein, the MRFF's president, warned that the reports indicate "the unrestricted euphoria of their commanders" who perceive a "'biblically-sanctioned' war that is clearly the undeniable sign of the expeditious approach of the fundamentalist Christian 'end times'" [5].

### The Official Level: Christian Nationalism in Policy

This is not limited to rogue commanders. US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, a former Fox TV host and National Guard officer known for his Christian nationalist rhetoric, has publicly framed Middle East policy in explicitly religious terms. He described the US as fighting "crazy regimes like Iran, hell-bent on prophetic Islamic delusions," which "cannot have nuclear weapons" [6].

The irony is lost on no one: American officials condemn Iran's "prophetic delusions" while their own commanders invoke Armageddon.

Hegseth's extremism is well-documented. He bears tattoos including a battle-cry phrase from the medieval Crusades, the Arabic word *kafir* (infidel), and a large cross across his chest symbolizing the medieval Kingdom of Jerusalem [6]. He has hosted monthly Christian worship series in the Pentagon and invited pastors who advocate for Christian theocracy [6].

### Direct Contradiction of Scripture

| What Scripture Commands | What American Commanders Do |
| :--- | :--- |
| "Watch" — remain alert but passive [1] | Launch first strike against Iran [5] |
| "No one knows the day or hour" [1] | Claim Trump is "anointed" to trigger Armageddon [5] |
| Attend to assigned tasks [1] | Frame war as fulfilling biblical prophecy [5] |
| Do not strike fellow servants [4] | Kill Iran's Supreme Leader and civilians [7] |
| Remain humble about divine timing [2] | Assert certainty about God's plan [5] |

## 3. How American Exceptionalism Creates an Environment of War

The current conflict is not an aberration but the logical outcome of a deeply embedded American ideology: **exceptionalism**.

### The "City on a Hill" Doctrine

The concept of American exceptionalism has deep roots in the country's history. As Columbia University Professor Jeffrey D. Sachs explains, "The United States of America has long viewed itself as an exceptional nation, even as God's New Israel, sent to redeem the world. This view has bipartisan support, with deep roots in the country's history, culture, and religious traditions" [8].

Ronald Reagan famously described America as "the shining city on the hill," harkening back to Puritan leader John Winthrop, who quoted Jesus (Matthew 5:14) in declaring the colonial settlements as "a city upon the hill, with the world's eyes upon it." Sachs notes that "American exceptionalism has been called the nation's civic religion" [8].

### The Consequences: Endless War

This self-perception has produced a specific pattern of behaviour. Historian Harry S. Stout has declared that "the norm of American national life is war," counting more than 280 "military interventions and nuclear standoffs on every corner of the globe" plus 29 wars with the country's indigenous peoples [8].

The tradition includes "finding divine purpose in war—to put, as Stout said, 'America's faith in the institution of war as a divine instrument and sacred mandate to be exercised around the world'" [8].

### Imperial Wars of Choice

Sachs argues that "American imperialism has always existed hand-in-hand with American exceptionalism. Since the idea of manifest destiny took hold in the nineteenth century, the United States has looked to expand its presence and influence not just from shore to shore across North America, but to nations around the world" [8].

The current conflict fits this pattern precisely. After World War II, the United States repeatedly took up the imperial mantle from European powers. In the Middle East, "the United States also took up the preceding wars of imperial Britain and France. America's motives were essentially the same: to secure Mideast oil and to project military power in Western Asia, the Eastern Mediterranean, and the Indian Ocean" [8].

### The Neoconservative Vision

The end of the Cold War unleashed an especially reckless phase. Former NATO Commander Wesley Clark described a conversation with Pentagon officials after the first Gulf War: "With the end of the Cold War, we can now use our military with impunity. The Soviets won't come in to block us. And we've got five, maybe 10, years to clean up these old Soviet surrogate regimes" [8].

This mindset produced wars in Afghanistan, Iraq, Syria, Libya, and now Iran—all framed in increasingly religious terms.

### The Role of Christian Zionism

The current administration has intensified the fusion of theology and policy. US Ambassador to Israel Mike Huckabee, an evangelical pastor, declared that Israel would be "justified in taking over" a vast stretch of the Middle East on biblical grounds, from the Nile to the Euphrates [9].

When asked whether Israel had a right to an area that, according to the Bible, covers "essentially the entire Middle East," Huckabee replied that "it would be fine if it took it all" [9]. These statements, based on a literal reading of Genesis 15, were condemned by Egypt, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, the UAE, and the Arab League as "a flagrant violation of the principles of international law and the Charter of the United Nations" [9].

The danger is clear: when theology becomes policy, compromise becomes heresy, and negotiation becomes betrayal [10].

## 4. Why America's Actions Cannot Be Considered Prophecy

The distinction between genuine prophecy and human planning is simple but profound.

### Prophecy Requires Waiting

As the Quran 21:37 commands: "Man was created of haste. I will show you My signs, so do not impatiently urge Me" [11]. This principle is echoed in Christian scripture: believers are to watch and wait, not to force God's hand [1].

A plan, by contrast, is when you act to make the prophecy come true. You launch the war. You build the temple. You prepare the red heifer. You tell troops they are "anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire." You are not waiting for God's timing; you are forcing your own.

### The Logical Test

If you are actively making it happen, it is not prophecy. It is a project.

| Party | Action | Claim | Verdict |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **US commanders** | Launch war; tell troops it's for Armageddon [5] | "God's plan" | **Plan disguised as prophecy** - they are the agents making it happen |
| **Hegseth/Huckabee** | Frame policy in biblical terms; advocate territorial expansion [6][9] | "Divine mandate" | **Plan disguised as prophecy** - using scripture to justify political goals |
| **Iran** | Defensive retaliation only; followed fatwa; did not initiate [7] | Mahdi will come in God's time | **Consistent with prophecy** - waiting, not forcing |
| **Gulf States** | Restraint under attack; diplomacy; no retaliation [12] | No prophecy claim made | **Humility** - no claim to know or control divine plan |

### The "Evil Servant" Warning Applied

Recall Jesus' warning about the servant who, believing the master delayed his coming, "shall begin to smite his fellow servants" (Matthew 24:48-49) [4]. The servant's error was not in his belief about the timing, but in his **action**—he used the perceived delay to justify striking others.

This is precisely what is happening today. American officials, believing they understand God's timeline, have launched a war that has killed thousands. They have struck Iran, killed its Supreme Leader, and dragged the region into conflict—all while claiming divine sanction.

### The Irony of "Prophetic" Certainty

Russell Moore, writing for Christianity Today, observed a troubling pattern: "When these things turn out not to be so clear after all, none of the prophecy teachers ever says, 'Well, I was wrong. Let me go back to the Scriptures and see where I failed.' They usually just move to the next confident set of assertions" [10].

More troubling still, Moore notes that "the answer to 'What will lead to the second coming of Christ?' always lines up with whatever their political tribe supports and can change as fast as that changes" [10].

This is not prophecy. It is politics wearing a robe.

### The Test of Authentic Faith

The Baptist Standard editorial board captured the proper response: "It never occurred to me when I was being taught dispensationalism as a child and teenager that that interpretation of Revelation would ever be official U.S. military doctrine. We ought to be concerned by such a hubristic assertion. And if a U.S. military commander really did instruct other officers to disseminate that claim, we ought to be concerned about how our military is being motivated to go into battle" [13].

The kingdom of God, Moore writes, "tells us what to care about (justice, peace, the poor, the vulnerable) while also shielding us from the disillusionment or bloodthirstiness that can come with expecting to have to bring the fullness of that kingdom on our own" [10].

## Conclusion: The Project Managers and Those Who Wait

The question at the heart of this investigation is simple: When someone claims to be fulfilling prophecy, are they witnessing divine will—or executing a human plan?

The evidence points to a clear answer. American actions in this conflict violate every principle of biblical eschatology. Scripture commands watchfulness; America launched a war. Scripture warns against striking others while waiting; America struck first. Scripture declares that no one knows the day or hour; American commanders claim certainty about God's timeline.

What we are witnessing is not prophecy. It is a plan—a political-military strategy baptized with scripture and sold as divine will. The "anointed" language, the Armageddon rhetoric, the territorial claims based on ancient texts—these are not signs of faith. They are signs of hubris.

The one who acts to force the end is not a prophet. They are a project manager.

And in 2026, the project managers are on one side—launching wars, building temples, preparing red heifers—while those who wait, defend, and restrain are on the other.

Which side actually trusts God's timing? The answer should be obvious to anyone who reads the text.
