# [Articles](/articles/) > [2026](/articles/2026/) > Project Management, Not Prophecy: How American Leadership Invoked and Violated the Scripture
 
**By Fasih Rana, March 12, 2026**
 
---
 
On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched a coordinated surprise attack on Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, his daughter, son-in-law, and grandson, along with the defense minister and the IRGC commander. Iranian state media confirmed at least 201 civilians killed across 24 provinces. Iran retaliated with missile and drone strikes on US bases, Gulf states, and Israel. [1]
 
American commanders told troops this was God's plan. Officials framed it in biblical terms. The Military Religious Freedom Foundation began receiving complaints within days, service members reporting that their commanders were citing the Book of Revelation and calling the war a step toward Armageddon. By March 2, they had documented more than 200 complaints across 50 installations. [2]
 
One question follows from all of this: does any of it align with what Christian scripture actually teaches?
 
Based on the words of Jesus himself, the answer is no. The men invoking scripture to justify this conflict are in direct violation of the scripture they invoke.

## Part One: What Christian Scripture Actually Commands
 
### 1.1 Watch, Wait, Do Not Force
 
In Mark 13, the disciples ask Jesus for a sign of the end, and his response establishes the governing principle:
 
> "No one knows about that day or hour, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father. Be on guard! Be alert! You do not know when that time will come. It's like a man going away: He leaves his house in charge of his servants, each with his assigned task, and tells the one at the door to keep watch. Therefore keep watch because you do not know when the owner of the house will come back — whether in the evening, or at midnight, or when the rooster crows, or at dawn. If he comes suddenly, do not let him find you sleeping. What I say to you, I say to everyone: Watch!" (Mark 13:32–37) [3]
 
The timing is withheld, from angels, from Jesus himself. The withholding is deliberate: the architecture of the teaching is that knowledge of the end is kept from every creature precisely so that no creature can calculate and act on it. The servants are assigned tasks and told to stay at their posts.
 
Luke sharpens this: "Take heed to yourselves, lest at any time your hearts be overcharged with surfeiting, and drunkenness, and cares of this life, and so that day come upon you unawares." (Luke 21:34) [4]
 
R.C. Sproul of Ligonier Ministries wrote that Jesus' own admission of not knowing the hour "is a warning to anyone who might try to set a date for His return," and that believers are "wasting time" trying to calculate it. [5] Acts 1:7 repeats the same principle at the close of Jesus' ministry: "It is not for you to know the times or dates the Father has set by his own authority." [6] The principle appears at the beginning and the end of Jesus' ministry, the same command repeated.
 
### 1.2 The Evil Servant
 
Matthew 24 goes further. Jesus describes a servant, not an unbeliever, someone inside the household of faith, who decides the master's delay is permission to act:
 
> "But and if that evil servant shall say in his heart, My lord delayeth his coming; And shall begin to smite his fellowservants, and to eat and drink with the drunken; The lord of that servant shall come in a day when he looketh not for him, and in an hour that he is not aware of, And shall cut him asunder, and appoint him his portion with the hypocrites: there shall be weeping and gnashing of teeth." (Matthew 24:48–51) [7]
 
The sin is presumption: the servant interprets the delay of the master's return as license to act, strikes his fellow servants, and ends up counted among the hypocrites.
 
This is the text held up to any commander who tells troops that launching a war is God's plan. The text condemns it directly.
 
### 1.3 Dispensationalism: From Recognizing to Forcing
 
The theology driving current US military and political circles is dispensational premillennialism, originating with John Nelson Darby in the nineteenth century, running through Hal Lindsey's *The Late Great Planet Earth* in 1970 and the *Left Behind* series after that.
 
Originally, dispensationalism held that believers *recognize* prophetic events, not *generate* them. But as the tradition fused with American exceptionalism, a more activist posture crept in. New Testament scholar Barbara Rossing notes that Darby assembled his system by stitching together disparate texts into a prophetic timetable Jesus himself never provided, and that Jesus "doesn't intend for us to piece together Bible verses to construct a detailed timetable." [8] The slide from recognizing to forcing is the logical consequence of that error: once you believe you can read the timetable, it is a short step to believing you can act on it.
 
The most prominent figure driving this fusion today is John Hagee, founder of Christians United for Israel. Before the February 28 strikes, Hagee told Fox News that Trump was uniquely suited to pursue a "biblically mandated policy" of cutting down Israel's enemies. [9] His son Matt Hagee framed the strikes directly: "God has a hook in the jaws of these nations, and he's drawing them here... He speaks about raining down fire and hail and brimstone. That's a heavenly air assault." [9] Days later, Hagee aired what was described as a pro-war infomercial before his sermon, with a narrator declaring "sirens are wailing, and prophecies written thousands of years ago are stepping onto the world stage." [10]
 
This is not new for Hagee. In *Jerusalem Countdown* he predicted Iran would have a nuclear bomb within two years. The prediction failed. He reissued the book in 2006 and made the same claim. It failed again. His 2013 "blood moon prophecy" predicted the lunar tetrad beginning April 2014 would signal the end times. The tetrad ended in September 2015. Nothing. [11] Russell Moore's observation fits precisely: "none of the prophecy teachers ever says, 'Well, I was wrong.'" [12] Each failure is absorbed and replaced with the next assertion. The pattern matters because it reveals the function of the language: motivating, not predictive.
 
The reach into policy is documented. CNN's Diana Bass puts it plainly: "There's almost a kind of spiritual eagerness for a war in the Middle East, because they believe a war is going to set off a series of events that will result in Jesus returning." [13] Ibrahim Abusharif at Northwestern University Qatar told Al Jazeera the end-times language is not incidental: it "activates a cultural script already present in American political theology," converting a geopolitical decision into a moral absolute. [14]
 
A belief system no event can falsify is not prophecy. It is ideology in prophetic costume.

## Part Two: American Exceptionalism and the Theology of War
 
### 2.1 The Plan Came First
 
One thing should be established before examining what these officials said: the scriptural justifications arrived after the policy. The policy itself grew from a thirty-year campaign.
 
Netanyahu's warnings on Iran's nuclear program form a documented record spanning three decades:
 
- **1992** — Knesset address: Iran "three to five years" from a nuclear weapon. [15]
- **1995** — *Fighting Terrorism*: same claim in print. [15]
- **1996** — Joint session of Congress: deadline "extremely close," consequences "catastrophic." [15]
- **2002** — Congressional testimony: Iran and Iraq both "racing to obtain nuclear weapons," the same year he was advocating for the invasion of Iraq, where subsequent inspections found no weapons of mass destruction. [15]
- **2009** — Iran months away. [15]
- **2012** — UN General Assembly: cartoon bomb, red line in marker, "by next spring, at most by next summer." The red line was never crossed. [15]
- **2015** — Congress again, opposing the JCPOA: Iran on "a pathway to the bomb." [15]
- **2025** — Months away, possibly weeks. The US Director of National Intelligence assessed Iran was not building a nuclear weapon. The strikes launched anyway. [15]
 
The pattern is identical to the one Russell Moore identified in the prophecy teachers: none of them ever says "I was wrong." The deadline is reissued. Its function is motivating, not predictive.
 
What makes the 2012 episode particularly sharp is what was happening inside Israeli intelligence at the same time. Leaked Mossad documents reported by Al Jazeera assessed that Iran at that moment was "not performing the activity necessary to produce weapons." The departing Mossad chief Meir Dagan had stated in his final intelligence summary that an Iranian nuclear weapon was not imminent, and that military action could actually accelerate Iran's weapons development rather than prevent it. Netanyahu's own intelligence agency contradicted him publicly. He continued anyway. [15]
 
In 2010 Netanyahu told The Atlantic that the danger was "a messianic apocalyptic cult controlling atomic bombs." The man who spent thirty years warning of Iran's messianic apocalyptic cult then partnered with officials invoking Armageddon theology, and with rabbis preaching that Netanyahu himself would "remain prime minister until the Messiah comes," to launch the war. [15]
 
Project 2025, the 900-page governing blueprint published in 2023 by the Heritage Foundation and written by the architects of the Trump administration, formalised what Netanyahu had been building for three decades. It describes Iran as being "at its weakest state in its history" and calls on the next Republican president to seize the moment to "free Iran." The language is entirely geopolitical. Regime change in Iran was a stated policy objective before a single verse was attached to it. [16]
 
The theology was added afterwards through a structured institutional mechanism. Russell Vought, one of Project 2025's primary architects, now Director of the Office of Management and Budget, helped revive the White House Cabinet Bible Study in Trump's second term, inviting new cabinet members to attend. The study is led by Ralph Drollinger of Capitol Ministries. Hegseth and Huckabee are listed as official sponsors. Speaker Mike Johnson attends. [16][17]
 
In June 2025, as Netanyahu was lobbying Washington to enter the war, Drollinger distributed a two-part lesson to his White House and congressional students titled "The Biblical Case for Defending Israel." His teaching: God blesses America's allies and curses its enemies. America must support Israel or face divine punishment. [17]
 
The sequence is documented. The plan was built over thirty years in the language of geopolitics. The theology was distributed in June 2025 to the men who would execute it. The strikes began in February 2026. What the commanders told their troops about Armageddon did not shape the policy. The policy was already written. The scripture was the costume it was given to wear.
 
### 2.2 The Tradition Behind the Contradiction
 
Pete Hegseth condemned Iran for harbouring "prophetic Islamic delusions." He said this while presiding over a military in which commanders were telling soldiers the war would bring Jesus back.
 
The contradiction has roots. Jeffrey D. Sachs at Columbia traces the tradition: the United States has long seen itself as "God's New Israel, sent to redeem the world," a view with "bipartisan support, with deep roots in the country's history, culture, and religious traditions." [18] Reagan invoked Winthrop's "shining city on a hill," itself from Matthew 5:14, as sacred mandate. Historian Harry S. Stout counted more than 280 American military interventions globally and documented "America's faith in the institution of war as a divine instrument." [18]
 
This tradition provides the soil in which dispensationalism took root, and the product of that combination is the expected outcome.
 
By March 2, 2026, it had produced this: more than 200 complaints from service members across 50 military installations, documented by the Military Religious Freedom Foundation. [2] A non-commissioned officer reported a commander stating: "President Trump has been anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon and mark his return to Earth." [2] Another reported their commander urging officers to tell troops this was "all part of God's divine plan" and "specifically referenced numerous citations out of the Book of Revelation referring to Armageddon and the imminent return of Jesus Christ." [2] MRFF president Mikey Weinstein described commanders experiencing the "unrestricted euphoria" of a "'biblically-sanctioned' war" representing "the expeditious approach of the fundamentalist Christian 'end times.'" [2]
 
### 2.3 The Official Level
 
This is not rogue subordinates. It runs to the top.
 
Hegseth bears tattoos of crusader iconography: *Deus Vult*, a Crusader cross representing the medieval Kingdom of Jerusalem. He has invited pastors who advocate Christian theocracy to lead worship at the Pentagon. [19]
 
Ambassador to Israel Mike Huckabee, an evangelical pastor, declared Israel would be "justified in taking over" the Middle East from the Nile to the Euphrates on biblical grounds, "it would be fine if it took it all." [20] Egypt, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, the UAE, and the Arab League condemned the statements as a "flagrant violation of the principles of international law." [20]
 
These statements came from the Secretary of Defense and the Ambassador to Israel, not from fringe voices within the administration.
 
Then there is Senator Ted Cruz, one of the most vocal congressional advocates for the war. In a June 2025 interview with Tucker Carlson, Cruz was asked the population of Iran. He didn't know. Carlson asked him to name Iran's major ethnic groups. Cruz came up blank. Carlson's response: "You're a senator who's calling for the overthrow of the government, and you don't know anything about the country." [21]
 
Cruz's justification for supporting the war was explicitly biblical. He told Carlson that since childhood he had been taught: "Those who bless Israel will be blessed, and those who curse Israel will be cursed." He wanted to be on the blessing side. When Carlson pressed him for the source, Cruz pointed to Genesis but couldn't locate the verse. [22]
 
The verse is Genesis 12:3. It reads: "I will bless those who bless you, and whoever curses you I will curse." The word Israel does not appear in it. It cannot; Jacob, who was later renamed Israel, had not yet been born. God is speaking to Abraham, who at that moment is being called to migrate into Canaan. The land is not yet called Israel. The nation does not yet exist. [22]
 
Gary Burge, PhD, formerly of Wheaton College and Calvin Theological Seminary, one of the most respected evangelical New Testament scholars in the United States, commented directly on the Cruz-Carlson exchange. His verdict: a "dreadful misunderstanding of the Bible." The promise "does not imagine a timeless provision for a political nation 4,000 years in the future." It provides assurance to Abraham and his family that they will establish themselves in the land. And it includes a blessing "to all the families of the earth," making it "not a prescription for ethnic nationalism" but a declaration that God's purposes extend beyond any single nation. [22]
 
Cruz is the pattern made visible: unable to name the population of the country he wants to topple, grounding his foreign policy in a verse that does not contain the word he is applying it to.
 
Cruz has company at that level. Senator Lindsey Graham, widely reported to be the most influential congressional voice behind the decision to strike, with the Wall Street Journal reporting he travelled to Israel multiple times to coach Netanyahu on how to lobby Trump to start the war [23], told reporters: "This is a religious war, and we will determine the course of the Middle East for a thousand years." [23] House Speaker Mike Johnson, third in line to the presidency, asserted that Iranians have a "misguided religion." [24] Secretary of State Marco Rubio declared the war necessary because Iran is "run by lunatics — religious fanatic lunatics." [24]
 
Graham's theological framing goes further than anyone else in this orbit. In August 2025 he declared: "If America pulls the plug on Israel, God will pull the plug on us." [23] On March 10, 2026, two days before this article was written, he appeared on Fox News and signed off with: "God bless Israel. We should move all our stuff to Israel." [23]
 
There is no verse behind either statement. Cruz at least cited a real passage, even if he misread it. Graham has invented a theological principle, divine punishment contingent on American foreign policy, and stated it as if it were scripture. It is not in the Bible anywhere.
 
Then, in the same period, Graham told Fox News that when Iran's regime falls "we are going to make a tonne of money," noting that Iran and Venezuela together hold 31 percent of the world's oil reserves and that America would have "a partnership" with those reserves. [23]
 
The sacred language and the material interest appeared together, in the same senator, in the same interviews: a religious war that would, by his own account, make a tonne of money. This is what ideology wearing prophetic costume looks like from the inside.

## Part Three: Islamic Eschatology, the Diplomatic Record, and the Isolation of the American Position
 
Both major Islamic traditions arrive, through entirely different routes, at the same structural prohibition Christian scripture establishes. In the Twelver Shia case it goes further: an explicit, named, doctrinal rule. And the Muslim-majority states most directly affected by this war, states that had every theological vocabulary available to frame it in religious terms, chose not to use it.
 
### 3.1 Sunni Islam
 
The Mahdi does not appear in the Quran. He is absent from Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, the two most authoritative Sunni hadith collections. He appears in secondary collections as a future figure, his timing unknowable, his arrival something to await rather than schedule.
 
For Salafi scholars, the dominant theological influence across much of the Gulf, eschatological speculation is itself suspect. The correct posture is watchfulness combined with present duty, which is structurally the same prohibition established in Mark 13.
 
The Gulf Arab states and Pakistan demonstrated exactly this in the current conflict. Subjected to spill over strikes, facing pressure from the United States and their own populations, Gulf governments and Pakistan's military chose deliberate restraint: de-escalation channels, no retaliation, no eschatological framing. No divine mandate claimed. No signal fires. Whatever their political calculations, their public theology was humble.
 
### 3.2 Twelver Shia Islam
 
Twelver Shia doctrine is structured around the occultation of the twelfth imam, Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Mahdi, who entered concealment in 874 CE and has been in Major Occultation since 941 CE. The imam is present but hidden, may not be represented by any human agent, and returns only when God appoints.
 
His final communique at the start of the Major Occultation declared that "whosoever declares otherwise is no less than an imposter." [25]
 
Classical Shia hadith collections explicitly prohibit two things: *este'jal* (haste) and *tawqit* (setting a time). [26] These are not recommendations. They are doctrinal guardrails, built in precisely because the temptation to accelerate is real.
 
The correct posture is *intizar*: patient, active waiting. A doctrine that structurally cannot generate the logic of "lighting a signal fire."
 
### 3.3 Quran 21:37
 
> *"Khuliqa al-insanu min 'ajal. Sa-urikum ayati fa-la tasta'jilun."*
> "Man was created of haste. I will show you My signs, so do not impatiently urge Me." (Quran 21:37) [27]
 
The verse was revealed to those demanding immediate divine punishment, urging God to act on their schedule. The Quran names the demand as a *flaw*. Haste is a human weakness; the Quran names it as such.
 
Classical tafsir is unified on this. Tafsir Maarif ul-Quran distinguishes blameworthy haste, demanding divine action before its time, from praiseworthy urgency in performing one's own commanded duties. [28] Maududi frames impatience as arrogance: placing human urgency above divine wisdom. [29] Al-Qurtubi is most precise: *'ajal* describes a constitutive weakness; man is so prone to haste it is as if he is made of it. The prohibition *fa-la tasta'jilun* is a direct command against demanding God accelerate what He has not yet appointed. His illustration is Badr: God did not send His signs when the mockers demanded them, but when He chose, and when He did, there was no reversing it. Signs belong to God's timeline. Demanding otherwise is to misunderstand who holds the command, and to rush God's signs is, by that understanding, the opposite of devotion. [30]
 
### 3.4 What Muslim States Actually Did
 
The states that bore the direct consequences of this war are among the most majority-Muslim countries on earth, struck by Iranian missiles, hosting US bases, absorbing the spill over of a conflict they did not start: Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan, Türkiye, Pakistan. Every one of them had access to a rich theological tradition with eschatological vocabulary. Not one of them used it.
 
Saudi Arabia and the UAE, both struck by Iranian missiles, both with powerful Salafi religious establishments, condemned the Iranian attacks in the language of international law and called for restraint. Neither invoked scripture. Neither framed the strikes as divine will or prophetic fulfilment. Saudi Arabia had been pursuing rapprochement with Iran for years before the war began, a policy its religious establishment supported in silence. [31]
 
Oman mediated the nuclear negotiations that were still active the night before the strikes began. Its foreign minister was briefing parties on potential breakthrough progress when the bombs fell. Oman's response was to warn the US against being "sucked in" further: the language of prudence, not prophecy. [32]
 
Türkiye, a NATO member whose airspace was violated by an Iranian missile, called the strikes illegal and immediately offered mediation. President Erdogan's framing was consistent throughout: "No results can be achieved as a result of these attacks. Diplomacy must be brought back into play." Türkiye's foreign minister spoke to fifteen counterparts in the first two days. Not one Turkish statement invoked religion. [31]
 
Pakistan, a nuclear-armed majority Muslim state whose citizens protested in the streets, whose Shia minority rioted, and whose Strategic Mutual Defence Agreement with Saudi Arabia was triggered, condemned both the US strikes and Iran's retaliation in the language of international law. Prime Minister Sharif offered to play "a constructive role in de-escalation and promoting dialogue," with no divine mandate claimed and no prophetic certainty invoked. [32]
 
Qatar and Egypt joined the mediation effort. Jordan, which holds the Hashemite custodianship of Islamic holy sites in Jerusalem, perhaps the most theologically loaded position in the Muslim world, called for de-escalation and respect for international law. [32]
 
These states are not theologically timid. They have scholars, mosques, and governments with centuries of Islamic jurisprudence available to them. They chose not to use it because their traditions, Sunni and Shia alike, do not sanction the presumption of divine timing. They practiced what their theology commands: restraint, watchfulness, diplomacy. *Intizar* in action, even without naming it.
 
### 3.5 The Isolation
 
The picture this produces is precise. Every major Islamic tradition with a stake in this conflict operates within a theological framework that prohibits the presumption of prophetic agency: Sunni, Shia, the states that were bombed, the states that were hit in the crossfire. None of them claimed it. None of them lit a signal fire.
 
The one party that did, that told its soldiers they were anointed to trigger Armageddon, that called it a thousand-year religious war, that invented scriptural principles to justify it, is the party whose own scripture most explicitly forbids it.
 
The American position violates Christian teaching; it stands condemned by every major theological tradition in this conflict. It stands alone, unsupported by the text it invokes, unsupported by the traditions of the region it struck, unsupported by the independent conclusions of every serious theological framework that has ever grappled with the question of human agency and divine timing.

## Part Four: Prophecy or Plan?
 
The position is isolated. But isolated from what, exactly? The isolation runs deeper than geography: not only from the traditions of the region they struck, but from their own. The question that remains is what to call what they are actually doing. There is a precise word for it.
 
### 4.1 The Distinction
 
Prophecy is something God does, and the human role is to receive it, interpret it, and wait; nothing more.
 
If you are the agent making it happen, it is not prophecy. It is a project.
 
When a commander tells troops they are "anointed to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon," he is describing an operation with a manager, a trigger, and an expected outcome. God in that description is not an actor. He is a reward: a validation of what humans have already decided to do.
 
No Christian tradition, including the most aggressive dispensationalism, has historically claimed the authority to *generate* prophetic events. Only to *recognize* them. The moment believers become the cause of what they claim to merely await, they have left the territory of prophecy entirely.
 
### 4.2 Matthew 24, Applied
 
The Evil Servant is condemned not for impatience but for converting the wait into license, deciding the master is delayed, so he may now smite.
 
American commanders, believing they understand God's timeline, launched a war. They called it prophetic. The text they invoke calls it something else.
 
The Baptist Standard was blunt: "It never occurred to me when I was being taught dispensationalism as a child and teenager that that interpretation of Revelation would ever be official U.S. military doctrine. We ought to be concerned by such a hubristic assertion." [33]
 
---
 
## Conclusion: Prophecy Is Not a Project Management Framework
 
American military and political leaders are invoking Christian scripture to justify a war. That same scripture, read plainly, forbids exactly what they are doing. It forbids claiming knowledge of God's timing. It forbids striking others while waiting. It condemns the servant who converts delay into license. It names impatience as a weakness. The men calling this war a fulfilment of prophecy are in direct violation of the prophecy they claim to fulfil. That violation sits on the face of the text, plain and unambiguous.
 
Every major Islamic tradition arrived at the same prohibition independently; the Muslim-majority states that bore the consequences of this war demonstrated it in practice, choosing diplomacy and restraint while the party invoking scripture chose neither.
 
The one who acts to force the end is not a prophet. They are a project manager. And project management is not what the text commands.
