## Why Judaism and Christianity Warn Against Hastening the End, But Only Islam Builds the Warning Around Human Nature
 
**By Fasih Rana, March 2026**
 
In the opening months of 2026, the United States and Israel launched a war against Iran that has drawn in the Gulf Arab states and reshaped the region. As American commanders told troops they were fighting for Armageddon and Israeli activists prepared red heifers on a West Bank hillside, Muslim-majority states on every side of the conflict chose restraint. Iran retaliated but did not initiate. The Gulf Arab states absorbed drone strikes on their territory without retaliating. Pakistan mediated with a nuclear arsenal it did not deploy. The companion article in this series, *[American Actions Contradict Scripture](https://tictactrinity.github.io/articles/2026/american_actions_contradict_scripture)*, examined how those American actions violate Christian scripture's own commands. This article asks the prior question: why does only one of the three Abrahamic traditions appear to produce restraint under these conditions, when all three contain some prohibition against forcing the messianic end?
 
**The answer lies in what each prohibition actually says, where in each tradition it sits, and what it assumes about the people it is addressing.**
 
---
 
## 1. The Claim Each Tradition Makes
 
A fair reading of all three traditions begins with what each actually contains.
 
### Judaism
 
The closest Jewish prohibition to Quran 21:37 is found in the Babylonian Talmud, tractate Ketubot 111a. The passage, drawn from an allegorical reading of the Song of Songs, enumerates three oaths sworn at the time of Israel's exile. Israel swore not to ascend to the Land en masse, as though storming a wall. Israel also swore not to rebel against the nations of the world. In turn, the nations swore not to oppress Israel beyond endurance. Taken together, these are known as the Shlosh Shevuot, the Three Oaths, and they have been cited for centuries as a prohibition against using force to end the exile and hasten the messianic age. [[1]](#1)
 
### Christianity
 
The Christian prohibition is found in the words of Jesus himself. In Mark 13:32-37, when his disciples ask for signs of the end, Jesus answers that no one knows the day or the hour, not the angels, not even the Son, but only the Father. His command is to watch, to remain alert, and to attend to assigned duties. [[2]](#2) In Matthew 24:48-51, Jesus warns against the servant who, believing the master has been delayed, begins to strike his fellow servants: that servant will be cut asunder and appointed his portion with the hypocrites. [[3]](#3) Luke 21:34 warns believers not to let their hearts be weighed down with the cares of this life, lest the day come upon them unawares. [[4]](#4)
 
### Islam
 
In Quran 21:37, God addresses humanity directly: "Man was created of haste. I will show you My signs, so do not impatiently urge Me." Classical commentators have understood this as a definitive prohibition. Shaykh Abdul-Rahim Reasat explains that the verse describes humanity as hasty and impatient, "as though he has been made from hastiness and that it is an essential part of him." The context, according to classical exegesis, is humanity's historical tendency to demand miracles and divine punishments instantly, and the verse answers that demand by commanding patience. [[5]](#5)
 
All three traditions, on the surface, point in the same direction. Whether they point with the same force is a separate question entirely.

## 2. Where Jewish Law Actually Stands
 
The Three Oaths carry significant theological weight in Jewish tradition, but their structural position within Judaism is considerably weaker than it first appears.
 
The oaths appear in the Talmud's *aggadic* section, narrative and homiletical literature distinguished from *halacha*, the binding legal code. Jewish law is built on halacha. Aggadic passages are valued for moral instruction, but they do not carry the same legal force as commandments derived from the Torah. The Three Oaths are not in the Torah. They are not in Levitical law. The concept of the Messiah does not appear in Mosaic law at all. The Torah focuses on covenant law, sacrifice, and communal ethics; the messianic concept developed in prophetic and later rabbinic literature, centuries after the Torah's composition. [[6]](#6)
 
This structural position has made the Three Oaths contestable in a way that a Torah commandment would not be, and they have been contested extensively.
 
Religious Zionist authorities beginning in the early twentieth century argued that the oaths were annulled by the nations' breach of their own obligation, because the Holocaust represented exactly the kind of oppression the nations had sworn not to commit. Rabbi Avraham Yitzchak HaCohen Kook, whose thought became foundational to the settler movement, held that secular Zionist immigration to Palestine did not constitute the forbidden ascending en masse, and that divine redemption was actively unfolding through the return to the land. His son, Rabbi Tzvi Yehuda Kook, went further, arguing that the establishment of Israel was itself a sacred act and that continued settlement was a religious obligation.
 
Within the Talmud itself, a competing tradition sits in Sanhedrin 98a. Rabbi Alexandri, cited there, taught that if the entire community of Israel were to repent for even a single day, the Messiah would come immediately. [[7]](#7) This is a tradition of hastening through righteous action, and it sits in the same body of rabbinic literature as the Three Oaths. Jewish tradition, in summary, contains a genuine restraint tradition and a genuine hastening tradition, and the dominant institutional and political force in Israeli religious life today has moved decisively toward the hastening faction.
 
The evidence from 2026 confirms this. In the occupied West Bank, five red heifers imported from Texas are being maintained in ritual purity, their ashes required according to Numbers 19 for the purification that would permit the construction of a Third Temple. At a February 2026 conference at the Shilo settlement, two opening speakers stood at the lectern with assault rifles slung around their shoulders. The Temple Institute, which organized the event, has been preparing ritual vessels, priestly garments, and architectural plans for decades. The plan requires the displacement or demolition of the Al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock, the third holiest site in Islam. [[8]](#8)

This preparation has a documented history of translating into violence, which is the standard by which preparation crosses into hastening. The Jewish Underground, a militant cell composed of senior Gush Emunim settler figures, carried out car bombings that maimed the mayors of Nablus and Ramallah in 1980, and a mass shooting at the Islamic College in Hebron in 1983 that killed three people and wounded thirty-three. The same cell developed an elaborate plan to bomb the Dome of the Rock, conceived from 1978 onwards and advanced to the point of stockpiling weapons and explosives before Shin Bet arrested its members in April 1984, foiling the attack before it could be executed. [[9]](#9) In 1994, Baruch Goldstein, a Kach movement member with ties to the Temple Mount movement, massacred twenty-nine Palestinian worshippers at the Ibrahimi Mosque in Hebron. The Israeli government's response was to divide the site permanently, with Palestinian Muslim access curtailed and a large portion converted into a Jewish prayer space under military control. [[10]](#10) In 2024, four men were arrested for planning to sacrifice a goat on the Temple Mount, in their own words, to incite fighting between Arabs and Jews.
 
Preparation, in this specific tradition, has a documented track record of producing attacks. The Three Oaths have not held. They were argued away in the twentieth century by the same institutions that now sit at the centre of the Israeli government.

## 3. Where Christian Scripture Actually Stands
 
The Gospel passages on the end times are genuine teachings on patience and humility. They address a question that is adjacent to Quran 21:37, but they address a different one.
 
Jesus, in Mark 13 and Matthew 24, is answering questions about signs and timing. His answer withholds information: no one knows the day or the hour. This is a statement about epistemic limits, about what believers cannot know, rather than a prohibition on what they must not do. The command that follows, "Watch," is a command to remain alert to what God is doing, not a command to refrain from hastening. The distinction is structural, and it carries significant weight in practice.
 
A prohibition based on unknowing is architecturally different from a prohibition based on knowing. Jesus says: you cannot know the hour, therefore watch. The verse does not say: you are hasty by nature, and because of that nature, I am commanding you not to rush Me. The flaw is not named. The specific impulse that the command is designed to restrain is not identified.
 
The Parable of the Evil Servant in Matthew 24 warns against the servant who uses the master's apparent delay as a licence to strike others. This is a moral warning against violence, but it is conditional on a particular failure mode rather than identifying a structural feature of human nature.
 
The gap left by this conditional framing was not without consequences in the tradition. The Apostle Peter, writing in 2 Peter 3:12, addresses believers who are "looking for and hastening the coming of the day of God." [[11]](#11) The word translated as "hastening" in the ESV, NIV, and NASB is the Greek *speudontas*, meaning to hurry or speed forward. Mainstream Reformed and evangelical commentators have read this not as a warning against hastening but as an endorsement of it, arguing that believers can accelerate the end through evangelism, holy living, and prayer. The verse is standard in dispensationalist theology, and it runs in a direction precisely opposite to restraint.
 
The practical outcome of this gap is visible in the present conflict. The Military Religious Freedom Foundation documented more than 200 complaints from service members across fifty military installations by March 2, 2026. [[12]](#12) One anonymous non-commissioned officer reported a commander stating that the war with Iran was "all part of God's divine plan," that the conflict would lead to Armageddon and the imminent return of Jesus Christ, and that "President Trump has been anointed by Jesus to light the signal fire in Iran to cause Armageddon and mark his return to Earth." These commanders are reading a real strand of their tradition. Christian scripture, taken as a whole, does not foreclose that reading.

## 4. The Structural Difference in Quran 21:37
 
The verse at the centre of this argument does something that neither the Three Oaths nor the Gospel passages accomplish.
 
It names the flaw first: "Man was created of haste." The Arabic *khuliq* carries the sense of being fashioned, constituted, made from something. Hastiness is not described as a sin to be overcome. It is described as a feature of human nature, an intrinsic tendency woven into what human beings are. The classical commentary is precise on this point: humans are hasty "as though he has been made from hastiness and that it is an essential part of him." [[5]](#5)
 
Having named the flaw, the verse then issues the command: "so do not impatiently urge Me." The word "so" connects the diagnosis directly to the prohibition. The command follows from the diagnosis. God says: because this is what you are, do not do this. The prohibition is calibrated to the specific weakness it is restraining, addressed to a species that the verse has already identified as constitutionally impatient.
 
This structure, a diagnosis followed by a command grounded in that diagnosis, has no equivalent in the passages surveyed above. The Three Oaths do not say: Israel is by nature impatient to return, therefore swear not to do so. Jesus does not say: you are creatures made of haste, therefore do not demand My signs.
 
The practical consequence is that the prohibition is designed to hold precisely when the human tendency it names is strongest. When a believer is most tempted to force God's hand, the verse speaks directly to that moment, acknowledging the temptation as natural and commanding against it on that basis. A restraint built around the named flaw is a restraint designed for the worst conditions. That, in 2026, is exactly what was tested.

## 5. The Evidence: Shia Iran, Sunni Arab States, and Pakistan
 
The 2026 conflict provides an opportunity to examine whether this structural difference has observable consequences. The evidence requires a careful distinction between Iran's Shia Islam and the Sunni Islam of the Arab Gulf states and Pakistan, because their eschatological frameworks differ significantly, and their restraint in this conflict came from different positions.
 
### Iran: Shia Restraint Under Direct Attack
 
Iran's dominant theological tradition is Twelver Shia Islam, which holds a specific and institutionalized doctrine about the return of the Hidden Imam, the Mahdi, whose reappearance will usher in the end of times. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps is constitutionally tasked with "paving the way for the Mahdi." [[13]](#13) This is an explicit, state-embedded eschatological mission, one that analysts have cited for years as evidence of potential Iranian aggression.
 
The events of 2026 tested whether that doctrine produced aggression in practice. On February 26, two days before the US-Israeli strike that killed Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, Iranian officials were in Geneva for indirect nuclear negotiations with the United States. Senior adviser Ali Shamkhani posted publicly that the non-development of nuclear weapons was fully consistent with Khamenei's fatwa prohibiting weapons of mass destruction. President Masoud Pezeshkian described claims that Tehran sought nuclear weapons as "lies." [[14]](#14)
 
When the strike came, Iran's response was retaliatory. It struck US and Israeli assets and hit Gulf infrastructure hosting American military operations. [[15]](#15) Iran did not launch a first strike. Despite holding an institutionalized apocalyptic theology far more developed than anything in Sunni eschatology, its conduct was defensive throughout.
 
### Sunni Arab States: Absorption Without Retaliation
 
The Gulf Cooperation Council states, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Bahrain, Qatar, Kuwait, and Oman, operated from a different theological framework. Sunni eschatology does not centre on the IRGC's specific Mahdist mandate, and the Gulf states carry no equivalent constitutional provision about hastening the end.
 
Hundreds of Iranian drones and missiles were intercepted across GCC territory. Civilian infrastructure was struck: airports, oil facilities, desalination plants. Three civilians were killed and 78 injured in the UAE alone. [[16]](#16) None of these states retaliated against Iran.
 
Anna Jacobs of the Arab Gulf States Institute observed that the countries of the region were "confronting a shared national security threat" and were "all signalling that they are working to find off-ramps and to de-escalate as much as they can." Ali Bakir of Qatar University noted that GCC calculations were "based on the understanding that they are equipped to defend themselves and can absorb the negative financial and economic implications of the conflict," and that they would "continue to urge all involved parties to halt hostilities and return to the negotiation table." [[16]](#16) Their restraint was substantive, sustained under direct attack, and oriented entirely toward diplomacy.
 
### Pakistan: Sunni Nuclear Power as Mediator
 
Pakistan adds a dimension that the Iranian and Gulf examples do not. It holds the world's fifth-largest nuclear arsenal. It is bound by a Strategic Mutual Defence Agreement with Saudi Arabia. It was directly implicated by the Gulf strikes.
 
Deputy Prime Minister Ishaq Dar told the Pakistani Senate on March 3 that Islamabad had been in close contact with multiple countries to encourage a return to dialogue. He revealed that Tehran had sought assurances that Saudi territory would not be used against Iran, and that he personally obtained those assurances from Riyadh. Chief of Army Staff Field Marshal Asim Munir halted his return journey from the United States to hold consultations in Türkiye, where the Pakistani delegation urged Iran to resolve matters through diplomacy. [[17]](#17)
 
Pakistan's Defence Minister Khawaja Asif stated publicly that the war was "orchestrated by the Zionists." [[18]](#18) The rhetoric was pointed. The response was mediation. Pakistan used its nuclear capability and its strategic position to create space for diplomacy rather than to escalate.

## 6. What the Pattern Shows
 
Placed side by side, the actions of all parties follow theological architecture more closely than they follow material interest.
 
The United States and Israel, the parties acting within traditions whose prohibitions on hastening are either absent from foundational law, contested within their own literature, or accompanied by competing texts that point in the opposite direction, launched the first strikes. Their commanders framed the war in explicitly apocalyptic terms. Their activists maintained red heifers and prepared Temple vessels. Their officials invoked biblical territorial claims encompassing the entire Middle East.
 
Iran, acting within a Shia framework that holds an explicit and institutionalized Mahdist doctrine, responded defensively and did not initiate. The Sunni Arab Gulf states, without that specific Mahdist mandate but with Quran 21:37 embedded in their foundational text, absorbed direct attacks on their territory and sought diplomacy. Pakistan, a Sunni nuclear power with treaty obligations in the region, mediated.
 
The common factor across all three Muslim actors, Shia Iran with its Mahdist constitution, the Sunni Arab states with their strategic calculations, and Sunni Pakistan with its nuclear arsenal, is the presence of Quran 21:37 in the text from which all of them draw. The verse does not appear in the Talmud. It does not appear in the words of Jesus. Its structural move, naming hastiness as an intrinsic feature of human nature and then commanding against it on that precise basis, has no equivalent in either tradition.
 
---
 
## The Waiting and the Planning
 
Judaism and Christianity contain genuine restraint traditions. The Three Oaths exist. The teachings of Jesus on watchfulness exist. They are real, and they have restrained real people at real moments in history. That record deserves acknowledgment.
 
The difference is that only one tradition embedded its restraint in a verse that first looks at human nature, names what it sees, and then issues its command on those grounds. A prohibition that accounts for the very tendency it restrains is a prohibition designed to hold when that tendency is strongest.
 
In 2026, the tendency was strongest of all. The region was at war. Apocalyptic expectations were running at a pitch not seen in living memory. Red heifers stood on a West Bank hillside. Commanders told soldiers they were lighting the signal fire for the return of Christ. Under those conditions, the restraint held on one side, and on the other it did not just collapse. It had been argued away long before the first missile was launched.
 
The verse that acknowledged humanity was made of haste was written for exactly that moment.
