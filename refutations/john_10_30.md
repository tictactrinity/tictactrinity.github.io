# [Refutations](/refutations/) >  What the Word "God" Erased: Reconstructing John 10:30-36 in Hebrew and Aramaic

Author: Fasih Rana  
Date: 13-July-2026  

## The passage as we normally read it

[John 10:22-30](https://biblehub.com/john/10.htm#22) is one continuous exchange. It opens with a direct question from the Jews gathered around Jesus at the Feast of Dedication: "How long will you keep us in suspense? If you are the Christ, tell us plainly" ([John 10:24](https://biblehub.com/john/10-24.htm)). Whatever Jesus says from that point on is his answer to that specific question — a claim to being the Christ, the one El Elyown sent and authorized, an elohim in the sense [Psalm 82](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82.htm) already established. It is this claim, not a separate or larger claim to being El Elyown himself, that the rest of the passage works out.

Here is [John 10:30-36](https://biblehub.com/john/10.htm#30), rendered plainly in English, the way most Bible readers encounter it:

> [30](https://biblehub.com/john/10-30.htm) "I and the Father are one." [31](https://biblehub.com/john/10-31.htm) The Jews picked up stones again to stone him. [32](https://biblehub.com/john/10-32.htm) Jesus said, "I have shown you many good works from the Father. For which of these are you stoning me?" [33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm) The Jews answered, "We are not stoning you for a good work, but for blasphemy — because you, a mere man, are making yourself God." [34](https://biblehub.com/john/10-34.htm) Jesus answered, "Is it not written in your Law, 'I said, you are gods'? [35](https://biblehub.com/john/10-35.htm) If he called them gods, to whom the word of God came — and Scripture cannot be broken — [36](https://biblehub.com/john/10-36.htm) do you say of the one whom the Father set apart as holy and sent into the world, 'You are blaspheming,' because I said, 'I am the Son of God'?"

This passage is a cornerstone of Trinitarian Christology. Jesus claims oneness with the Father; the crowd hears a claim to deity; Jesus defends himself by appealing to [Psalm 82](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82.htm). On the surface, he seems to be arguing toward the same conclusion by another route, that he shares the divine nature.

But every "God" in that paragraph is masking at least two different Hebrew words. Jesus is actively distinguishing them, not blurring them. To see that, we need to recover the Hebrew categories behind the Greek text.

## Where "elohim" comes from

**[Psalm 82:6](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82-6.htm)**, which Jesus quotes in [verse 34](https://biblehub.com/john/10-34.htm), reads:

**אֲנִי־אָמַרְתִּי אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם וּבְנֵי עֶלְיוֹן כֻּלְּכֶם**

*"I said, you are elohim, and bene elyown [sons of the Most High], all of you."*

The Psalm addresses corrupt human judges. God (El Elyown) stands above them in judgment. The whole Psalm depends on that separation. These **elohim** are not **El Elyown**; they are his delegated agents.

This usage is not isolated. It is a pattern across Torah and the Prophets.

**[Exodus 7:1](https://biblehub.com/exodus/7-1.htm)**: *YHWH* tells Moses, "See, I have made you elohim to Pharaoh." Moses speaks and acts with God's delegated authority.

**[Exodus 21:6](https://biblehub.com/exodus/21-6.htm)** and **[22:8-9](https://biblehub.com/exodus/22.htm#8)**: Israel's judges are called elohim because they administer God's justice.

**[1 Samuel 28:13](https://biblehub.com/1_samuel/28-13.htm)**: The witch of Endor, seeing Samuel raised, exclaims, "I see an elohim coming up out of the earth." Samuel is dead, not divine, yet the term applies.

**[Psalm 8:5](https://biblehub.com/psalms/8-5.htm)** [(Hebrew 8:6)](https://www.sefaria.org/Psalms.8.6?lang=bi): Human beings are made "a little lower than elohim," translated "angels" in some versions, but the Hebrew reads elohim.

**[Psalm 97:7](https://biblehub.com/psalms/97-7.htm)** and **[138:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/138-1.htm)**: Even foreign idols and pagan kings are called elohim in Hebrew scripture, not because they are the true God, but because they occupy positions of perceived divine authority.

The term elohim, then, is not a fixed ontological category. It is applied to authorized human agents (Moses, the judges), the dead (Samuel), false gods (idols, foreign kings) — and, in **[Psalm 82:1](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82.htm#1)** itself, to God directly: "Elohim stands in the divine assembly; among the elohim he holds judgment." The judge and the judged share the same word in the same psalm. Elohim alone, in other words, is genuinely ambiguous. It cannot settle by itself whether Jesus is claiming to be El Elyown or merely one of his delegated agents.

That is exactly why the term doing the real disambiguating work is **bene elyown**, not **elohim**.

## The grammar of verse 33

The underlying Greek accusation reads *poieis seauton theon*, "you make yourself *theon*," without the definite article. In predicate constructions where the subject is a person, anarthrous *theos* functions qualitatively: "you make yourself out to be *god / divine*," not an identification with the one true God. The crowd's charge is categorical, not definite. They are accusing Jesus of claiming the status of an *elohim*, not of being *El Elyown*.

The Greek grammar does not settle the question by itself, but it is consistent with the Hebrew framework Jesus invokes two verses later. The real work of establishing which category Jesus is claiming happens in Hebrew, in the terms he himself reaches for: *elohim* for the accusation, *bene elyown* for his defense.

## The reconstruction

If we map the Greek back to the Hebrew concepts underlying Jesus' appeal to Torah, the passage reads:

> **[27](https://biblehub.com/john/10-27.htm)** "My sheep hear My voice (those who are Mine listen to what I say and follow My commands/message) and I know them, and they follow Me. **[28](https://biblehub.com/john/10-28.htm)** I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of My hand. **[29](https://biblehub.com/john/10-29.htm)** My Abba, who has given them to Me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of Abba's hand. **[30](https://biblehub.com/john/10-30.htm)** I and Abba are one."

> **[31](https://biblehub.com/john/10-31.htm)** At this, the Jews again picked up stones to stone Him. **[32](https://biblehub.com/john/10-32.htm)** But Jesus responded, "I have shown you many good works from Abba. For which of these do you stone Me?"

> **[33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm)** "We are not stoning You for any good work," said the Jews, "but for blasphemy, because You, who are a man, make Yourself out to be Elohim."

> **[34](https://biblehub.com/john/10-34.htm)** Jesus replied, "Is it not written in your Torah: 'I have said, you are elohim'? **[35](https://biblehub.com/john/10-35.htm)** If he called them elohim — those to whom the word of Elohim/El Elyown came — and the Scripture cannot be broken — **[36](https://biblehub.com/john/10-36.htm)** then what about the One whom Abba set apart as holy and sent into the world? How then can you accuse Me of blasphemy for saying, I am Bene Elyown?"

## Why the Trinitarian reading fails

**[Verse 30](https://biblehub.com/john/10-30.htm)**: "One" is functional, not ontological. John could have written *heis* (masculine, personal oneness) but chose *hen* (neuter, unity of action and purpose). This is the same word Jesus uses in [John 17:22-23](https://biblehub.com/john/17.htm#22) for the disciples' unity with him and the Father: "that they may be one, even as we are one." If *hen* means shared divine essence in [10:30](https://biblehub.com/john/10-30.htm), the disciples share that essence too, which no Trinitarian accepts. The grammar points to relational and operational unity, not ontological identity. A son working in lockstep with his father is "one" with him functionally. He is still not his father.

**[Verse 33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm)**: The charge is elohim, not El Elyown. The crowd accuses Jesus of making himself *theon* without the definite article, which leaves room for a narrower reading than "claiming to be the one true God." They are using the same term Torah applies to Moses and the judges. Jesus' response does not deny the category; he legitimizes it by showing Scripture already applies it to others. The form of the argument matters: he is defending himself by narrowing the accusation, not broadening it into a claim to be the Most High.

**[Verses 34-36](https://biblehub.com/john/10.htm#34)**: The defense only works with separation. Jesus places himself in the lineage of those to whom "the word of El Elyown came," Moses, the judges, the *bene elyown* of [Psalm 82](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82.htm). He is the One "whom Abba set apart as holy and sent into the world." The sender and the sent remain two. The son and the father remain distinct. Jesus claims the title *Bene Elyown*, not *El Elyown*.

This is also why the closing phrase is "Son of God" and not some construction built on elohim alone. Elohim is a functional, shareable category; applied to judges, to the dead, even to idols; and one cannot meaningfully be "the son of" a shareable category. *Elyown*, by contrast, is the personal, singular referent [Psalm 82](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82.htm) itself uses in *bene elyown*. Whoever "Son of God" names, the referent has to be *Elyown* specifically, since sonship requires a person to be son of, not a title. The grammar of sonship itself forces the distinction: to be *Bene Elyown* is, by definition, not to be *Elyown*.

**[Psalm 82:7](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82-7.htm)** sharpens this further. Having called the judges *elohim* and *bene elyown* in [verse 6](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82-6.htm), the psalm pronounces their sentence in the very next verse: "nevertheless, you will die like men, and fall like any ruler." *Bene elyown*, as the psalm itself defines them, are mortal. They die like men; they fall like rulers. *El Elyown* is the one pronouncing that sentence from a position no death or fall touches. This is not a title distinction that could be dismissed as merely honorific — it is a difference in fate. Jesus, in claiming the title *bene elyown*, claims a category the psalm has already marked as mortal, and he would go on to die. That fits the *bene elyown* he identified himself with. It does not fit the *El Elyown* he never claimed to be.

## Active evidence of subordination

The argument so far has shown that John [10:30-36](https://biblehub.com/john/10.htm#30) does not require co-equality. But the Gospel provides positive evidence against it.

**[John 10:29](https://biblehub.com/john/10-29.htm)**: "My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all." Jesus says this immediately before "I and the Father are one." The Father's greatness is the ground of their unity, not its obstacle. They are one because the Father is greater and the Son operates under his authority. The oneness is functional alignment, not shared ontological status.

**[John 14:28](https://biblehub.com/john/14-28.htm)**: "The Father is greater than I." Jesus states this plainly, without Trinitarian qualification. If the Father is greater, the Son is not co-equal. This is not a temporary economic arrangement; it is Jesus' own description of his relationship to the Father. The one who is greater sends; the one who is lesser is sent. The one who is greater gives; the one who is lesser receives. This is the structure of [John 10:29-36](https://biblehub.com/john/10.htm#29) in miniature.

These verses do not coexist with co-equality. They override it.

## Objections

"But [John 5:18](https://biblehub.com/john/5-18.htm) says the Jews understood Jesus as 'making himself equal with God.'"

This objection collapses on three grounds.

First, the Gospel of John is a retroactive composition. The narrator is reconstructing scenes with full knowledge of later events. The "Jews" in [5:18](https://biblehub.com/john/5-18.htm) are characters in his narrative, not independent witnesses. Using [5:18](https://biblehub.com/john/5-18.htm) to validate [10:33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm) is circular: the same author put both accusations in their mouths.

Second, Jesus' own words in [John 5:19](https://biblehub.com/john/5-19.htm) and [5:30](https://biblehub.com/john/5-30.htm) raise a further problem for a co-equal reading of the Son. In [5:19](https://biblehub.com/john/5-19.htm): "the Son can do nothing of his own accord, but only what he sees the Father doing." In [5:30](https://biblehub.com/john/5-30.htm): "By myself I can do nothing... I seek not my own will but the will of him who sent me." Both describe an elohim carrying out the will of the one who sent him, not a co-equal actor with independent will. For a fuller treatment of the difficulty this creates for Trinitarian theology, see [Son the Mimic](https://tictactrinity.github.io/dilemmas/son_the_mimic).

Third, even within the narrative's own chronology, [5:18](https://biblehub.com/john/5-18.htm) occurs before [10:33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm). The accusation hasn't happened yet when we reach [chapter 10](https://biblehub.com/john/10.htm). But more importantly, the author is writing after all of it. He is not reporting what the Jews said; he is constructing what they said to serve his theological purposes. Those purposes are better read as highlighting the misunderstanding of Jesus' claims rather than endorsing it.

John [20:28](https://biblehub.com/john/20-28.htm): Thomas addresses Jesus as "My Lord and my God." This verse sits outside the scope of this argument. It isn't a citation of a fixed text the way [Psalm 82:6](https://biblehub.com/psalms/82-6.htm) is, and resolving it would require speculating about Thomas's state of mind rather than analyzing words Jesus himself chose. The case made here rests on John [10:22-36](https://biblehub.com/john/10.htm#22), [John 5:19](https://biblehub.com/john/5-19.htm) and [5:30](https://biblehub.com/john/5-30.htm), [John 14:28](https://biblehub.com/john/14-28.htm), and [John 17:3](https://biblehub.com/john/17-3.htm) — not on [20:28](https://biblehub.com/john/20-28.htm).

## Conclusion

The Trinitarian reading requires *elohim* and *El Elyown* to converge in Jesus. But Jesus' own defense depends on keeping them distinct. He is *bene elyown*, not *El Elyown*. He is the sent, not the sender. He is one with Abba in purpose and action, not in being.

[John 10:29](https://biblehub.com/john/10-29.htm) says the Father is greater than all. [John 14:28](https://biblehub.com/john/14-28.htm) says the Father is greater than the Son. [John 17:3](https://biblehub.com/john/17-3.htm) says the Father alone is the only true God. These are not marginal verses; they are Jesus' own words about his own relationship to the Father.

Once "God" stops standing in for both *elohim* and *El Elyown*, the reading that requires them to be the same collapses. In the words he actually reaches for, Jesus keeps the son and the father two separate beings, united in mission, separated in identity.
