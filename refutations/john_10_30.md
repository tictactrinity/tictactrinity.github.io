# [Refutations](/refutations/) >  What the Word "God" Erased: Reconstructing John 10:30-36 in Hebrew and Aramaic

## The passage as we normally read it

Here is John 10:30-36, rendered plainly in English, the way most Bible readers encounter it:

> [30](https://biblehub.com/john/10-30.htm) "I and the Father are one." [31](https://biblehub.com/john/10-31.htm) The Jews picked up stones again to stone him. [32](https://biblehub.com/john/10-32.htm) Jesus said, "I have shown you many good works from the Father. For which of these are you stoning me?" [33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm) The Jews answered, "We are not stoning you for a good work, but for blasphemy — because you, a mere man, are making yourself God." [34](https://biblehub.com/john/10-34.htm) Jesus answered, "Is it not written in your Law, 'I said, you are gods'? [35](https://biblehub.com/john/10-35.htm) If he called them gods, to whom the word of God came — and Scripture cannot be broken — [36](https://biblehub.com/john/10-36.htm) do you say of the one whom the Father set apart as holy and sent into the world, 'You are blaspheming,' because I said, 'I am the Son of God'?"

Read this way, the passage is a favorite proof-text for the deity of Christ. Jesus claims oneness with the Father; the crowd hears that as a claim to *be* God; and rather than deny it, Jesus seems to argue his way toward the same conclusion by another route. On the surface, it looks like Jesus doubling down.

But every "God" in that paragraph is doing the work of at least two different Hebrew words, words Jesus is actively distinguishing, not blurring. To see that, we need to look at where those words come from before reconstructing what Jesus is likely to have actually said.

## Showing the work: where "elohim" comes from

**[Psalm 82:6](https://biblehub.com/psalm/82-6.htm)** is the text Jesus quotes in verse 34. In Hebrew it reads:

**אֲנִי־אָמַרְתִּי אֱלֹהִים אַתֶּם וּבְנֵי עֶלְיוֹן כֻּלְּכֶם**

*"I said, you are elohim, and bene elyown [sons of the Most High], all of you."*

The Psalm addresses human judges, corrupt rulers God (El Elyown) is rebuking, and calls them both **elohim** ("gods") and **bene elyon** ("sons of the Most High," sons of *El Elyown*). Both titles describe people who carry God's delegated authority. Neither collapses them into being El Elyown himself; the whole Psalm depends on that separation, since God is rebuking these "elohim" from a position above them.

This isn't an isolated usage. In **[Exodus 7:1](https://biblehub.com/exodus/7-1.htm)**, *YHWH* tells Moses: "See, I have made you elohim to Pharaoh." Moses is called elohim, not because he shares God's being, but because he speaks and acts with God's delegated authority before Pharaoh. Israel's judges receive the same title in **[Exodus 21:6](https://biblehub.com/exodus/21-6.htm)** and **[22:8-9](https://biblehub.com/exodus/22.htm#8)**, again tied to their role in administering God's justice, not to any claim of divine identity.

So by the time Jesus reaches for [Psalm 82](https://biblehub.com/psalm/82.htm) in his own defense, "elohim" already has an established, concrete meaning in Torah: an authorized agent of El Elyown, distinct from El Elyown.

## A grammatical clue in verse 33 itself

Even the accusation in verse 33 supports this. The underlying Greek (*poieis seauton theon*) uses *theos* without the definite article — a construction closer to "you make yourself *a* god" than "you make yourself *the* God." That's the same anarthrous grammar debated in John 1:1. The crowd's own charge, in its original grammar, is closer to accusing Jesus of claiming to be *an* elohim than of claiming to be El Elyown.

## The reconstruction

Putting these pieces back where they belong — Abba for the intimate "Father," Torah for "Law," and elohim / El Elyown / Bene Elyown left untranslated where Jesus is drawing directly on Psalm 82 — the passage reads like this:

**[27](https://biblehub.com/john/10-27.htm)** "My sheep hear My voice (those who are Mine listen to what I say and follow My commands/message) and I know them, and they follow Me. **[28](https://biblehub.com/john/10-28.htm)** I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of My hand. **[29](https://biblehub.com/john/10-29.htm)** My Abba, who has given them to Me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of Abba's hand. **[30](https://biblehub.com/john/10-30.htm)** I and Abba are one."

**[31](https://biblehub.com/john/10-31.htm)** At this, the Jews again picked up stones to stone Him. **[32](https://biblehub.com/john/10-32.htm)** But Jesus responded, "I have shown you many good works from Abba. For which of these do you stone Me?"

**[33](https://biblehub.com/john/10-33.htm)** "We are not stoning You for any good work," said the Jews, "but for blasphemy, because You, who are a man, make Yourself out to be Elohim."

**[34](https://biblehub.com/john/10-34.htm)** Jesus replied, "Is it not written in your Torah: 'I have said, you are elohim'? **[35](https://biblehub.com/john/10-35.htm)** If he called them elohim — those to whom the word of Elohim/El Elyown came — and the Scripture cannot be broken — **[36](https://biblehub.com/john/10-36.htm)** then what about the One whom Abba set apart as holy and sent into the world? How then can you accuse Me of blasphemy for saying, I am Bene Elyown?"

## How this dissolves the Trinitarian reading

Once the terms are restored, the Trinitarian case built on this passage loses its footing at every step:

- **Verse 30** says "I and Abba are one" — the intimacy of a son with a father, not a claim of shared being. A son in unity with his father is still not his father.
- **Verse 33's accusation** is "Elohim," not "El Elyown", which the underlying Greek grammar already signals "a god," not "the God." The charge itself when correctly read is narrower than "claiming to be God" in the fullest sense.
- **Verses 34-36** only work as a defense because elohim and Bene Elyown are categories Torah already uses for people distinct from El Elyown; like Moses and the judges. Jesus isn't claiming to be El Elyown by another route; he's placing himself in the same category Moses and the judges occupied: one sent and set apart by Abba (El Elyown), authorized to speak and act in his name, but not identical to him.

Once "God" stops standing in for both Elohim and El Elyown, the reading that requires them to be the same collapses. In the words that he actually reaches for, Jesus keeps the son (Bene Elyown) and the father (El Elyown) two separate beings.
